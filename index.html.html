Basic Terms and Terminologies of Stock Market

Introduction

A stock market [3], is the aggregation of buyers and sellers of stocks or shares, which represent ownership claims on businesses; these may include securities listed on a public stock exchange as well as those only traded privately.

Classifications [4]:

  1. Stocks
  2. Bonds
  3. Security
  4. Derivatives

 

Stocks

Stocks are the shares of any individual company that are sold on the open market in a process called “Initial Public Offering (IPO)”.

When anyone buys a stock or an equity of the company, it means a small ownership is bought in that particular company

 

Bonds

 It represents the sum of money that is owed or due. When any government or corporative body needs to raise cash then they borrow money in the public market and subsequently pays interest on that loan to investors.

Stocks are preferred by those who have a long-term investment horizon and more tolerance for short-term market risks.

Security

These are the tradable financial assets.

Securities are classified into three categories:

  1. Debt Securities
  1. Bank Notes
  2. Government Bonds
  3. Debentures
  1. Equity Security
  1. Common Stock
  1. Derivatives
  1. Forward
  2. Futures
  3. Options
  1. Call
  2. Put
  1. Swaps

Debt Security

                                It may be called debentures, bonds, deposits, notes or commercial paper depending on their maturity, collateral and other characteristics. The holder of a debt security is typically entitled to the payment of principal and interest, together with other contractual rights.

  • Bank Notes

Type of negotiable promissory note made by the bank.

  • Government Bonds

These are medium or long-term debt securities issued by a sovereign government or their agencies. They carry a lower rate of interest than corporate bonds.

  • Debentures

A medium to long-term debt instrument used by large companies to borrow money, at a fixed rate of interest.

Equity Security

  • Common Stock

This type of share gives the shareholder the right to share in the profits of the company and to vote on policy matters.

Derivatives

It is a contract that derives its value from the performance of an underlying entity which can be an asset, index or interest rate. It can be used for a number of purposes including:

  • Insuring against price movement and speculation.
  • Getting access to hard-to-trade assets or markets.
  • Forwards

It is a contract between two parties for buying or to sell an asset at a specified future time at an agreed future price. For buying the underlying, the party assures the future in a long position and for selling in a short position.

  • Futures

It is a legal agreement to buy or sell something at a pre-determined price at a specified future time.

  • Options

It is contract price which gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a specified strike price on a specified date, depending on the form of an option.

Exchange [5]

It is an organized financial market where shares are bought and sold at prices governed by the forces.

Stock exchanges basically serve as

(1) primary markets where corporations, governments, municipalities, and other incorporated bodies can raise capital by channeling savings of the investors into productive ventures

(2) secondary markets where investors can sell their securities to other investors for cash, thus reducing the risk of investment and maintaining liquidity in the system.

The two famous Stock Exchange of India are:

  1. Bombay Stock Exchange
  2. National Stock Exchange

Bombay Stock Exchange [6]

The Bombay Stock Exchange is an Indian stock exchange established in 1875, it is Asia’s first stock exchange and claims to be the world's fastest stock exchange. It is the world's 10th largest stock exchange with an overall market capitalization of more than $2.3 trillion.

Sensex [7]

The S&P BSE SENSEX (S&P Bombay Stock Exchange Sensitive Index), is a market-weighted stock market index of 30 well-established and financially sound companies listed on Bombay Stock Exchange.

National Stock Exchange [8]

The National Stock Exchange of India Limited established in 1992 is the leading stock exchange of India.It came up as the first demutualized electronic exchange in the country.

Nifty [9]

The NIFTY 50 index is NSE’s benchmark stock market index for the Indian equity market. Full form of NIFTY is National Stock Exchange Fifty. It is one of the two main stock indices used in India.

Candle Stick Graph Presentation

A Candlestick graph consists of several candles which form several patterns depending upon the Open, High, Low, Close or OHLC prices. A Candle in a graph is a block if the stock closed lower the candle is colored black or red if the stock closes higher the candle is colored green or white.

A Candle Stick graph mainly represents two patterns:

  • Bullish Pattern

When there is a buying pressure, where the open price is lower than the close price of the stock. The green candle in the above image shows Bullish Candle, but there is a condition that a bullish pattern must form with a downtrend otherwise is a continuous pattern.

  • Bearish Pattern

When there is a selling pressure when the close price is lower than the open price of the stock. The red candle in the above image shows Bearish Candle.

The Candles depict the mood of the market. On the basis of some patterns like Hammer, Morning Star, Three White Soldier (shows stock is too strong) etc.

Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP)

The average prices based on the closing prices don’t provide an accurate picture of the stock mood. It identifies true avg. price for a period of time by multiplying the number of shares bought with share price and dividing this value from the total no of shares bought.

The equation to calculate VWAP over a period of time is as follows:

VWAP= no.of shares bought X share priceTotal shares bought

Spread High-Low/Bid-Ask

The difference between high and low prices of a stock or the spread between the high and low in a candle formed on a candlestick graph is known as Spread High Low.

This value helps an investor identify when to invest and when to sell. In other words, an investor can bid if the value is low in comparison to previous values and vice-versa.

The values for a negative change and a positive change is always positive. This graph can be used to monitor returns and liquidity of a stock price.

Moving Averages

The moving average is formed by calculating the average price of a stock price over a period of time. Mostly, these values are based on closing prices. The 5-day moving average is the five-day sum of closing prices divided by five. From its name, it is clear that a moving average is an average that moves along with time.

In the project, there is the calculation of moving averages over a span of 5 days, 10 days, 15 days and 20 days.

                    8.4     Ordinary Least Square Model (OLS) [13]

It is also known as linear least squares model. It is used for estimation of parameters which are unknown in any linear regression model. OLS chooses the parameters from the dataset by reducing the sum of the squares of the differences between the observed dependent variable to those predicted by the chosen linear function. Geometrically, it is observed as the smaller the differences between the sum of squared values, the better the model fits the data.
